Bounce


In this tutorial, we are going to cover an intermediate strafing technique called 'Bounce'. Bounce is a concept that is only seemingly perfectible by a robot and many people would improve their surf by working on the level of Bounce in their strafes. Many of the fastest 'unit' surfers and B-Hoppers demonstrate a clear understanding of the concept and have implemented it into their muscle memory. We will cover a basic explanation of the 'Bounce' concept, and you can try the exercises to implement more Bounce into your strafes!

What is 'Bounce'?

Bounce refers to the movement of the mouse when you change direction in a strafe. So, if you are strafing to the right and want to strafe to the left shortly after; Bounce refers to the transition period between those 2 strafes. There are 2 variables that decide the 'level' of Bounce in a strafe: Acceleration (and deceleration) and Pause.


Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change in movement over time, which includes a negative acceleration (like slowing down a movement). When we decide to strafe in the opposite direction our mouse is currently moving, we have to decelerate the mouse until it is at a complete stop, then accelerate the mouse in a new direction.
-- (This is when isolating away vertical mouse movements. If the mouse moves vertically when horizontal speed is at 0, the mouse will not be at a complete stop. Many B-hoppers have vertical movement in their Bounce). --


Pause

This, quite simply, refers to the amount of time the mouse is in a static state on the horizontal axis.


If a strafe transition has a low amount of acceleration and a long pause, it is said to have a low level of Bounce.
If a strafe transition has a high amount of acceleration and a short pause, it is said to have a high level of Bounce.

Sometimes we may be in circumstances which mean we have to have sub-optimal rates of acceleration, which will mean the quality of the Bounce will be largely determined by the length of the pause.

When a strafe transition has a low level of bounce, the strafes will look 'Swooshy' as opposed to a robotic, 'clicky' like appearance.

Ideally, our strafe transitions would have no discernible rate of Acceleration and no Pause, just an instant change of direction of mouse movement, synchronised with the strafing key. However, human muscular-skeletal ability and real world physics prevents us from achieving this. Even the greatest strafers have a short pause to allow them to change key before they move the mouse in the opposite direction, allowing for less error in synchronisation.

Why is Bounce important?

In Source-Mod Movement game modes, you gain speed while rotating at a certain, optimal, rate. We need to maximise the amount of time that we spend rotating at this rate and not at a sub-optimal rate (during deceleration, our rate will be sub-optimal).

The less time spent rotating, like the time spent paused, the less units we will gain.
The less time spent at a sub-optimal rate of rotation, the less units we will gain.

So, it is important to maximise our Bounce to maximise our unit gain from our strafes.

How to practice?

Most people can improve their Bounce simply by surfing and consciously thinking about the act of Bouncing. However, I have found it helps to Isolate the concept and practice moving the mouse horizontally, back and forth, while not surfing. I find it even more useful to have a visual aid, like to markers, that I can turn my view back and forth between, using the cross hair to guide my rotation.
If you spend 10-30 minutes a day just practicing your bounce as an intermediate, you will certainly have a significant improvement in the amount of units you gain from all strafes, especially strafes from a starting platform!
